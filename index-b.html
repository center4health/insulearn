What is diabetes?

“Diabetes is a condition that occurs when your blood sugar (blood glucose) is too high”. The units for glucose are mg/dL or mmol/L.


There are two main types of Diabetes:

  • Type 1 Diabetes: Not producing enough insulin to keep blood sugar in a normal range
  • Type 2 Diabetes: Producing Insulin, but not responding to it well

What is Insulin?

Insulin is a hormone made by your body in order to use sugar (glucose) from the food that you eat to create energy, or to store glucose for future use.


Why Insulin?

Insulin helps keep your blood sugar level from getting too high (hyperglycemia) or too low (hypoglycemia). If you are prescribed insulin, it may be because your body doesn't produce enough of it (type 1 diabetes) or your body doesn't use it effectively (type 2 diabetes).


Types of Insulin


There are different types of insulin you can use. The four main types are:

  • rapid-acting
  • short-acting
  • intermediate-acting
  • long-acting

Each type has a different:

  • onset: how quickly they start working after injection
  • peak: when they're most effective, and
  • duration: how long they're working in your body

Basal insulin (Intermediate-Acting and Long-Acting/ Background Insulin) regulates blood sugar levels and is slowly absorbed throughout the day to keep levels constant while fasting. It is taken once or twice a day and is taken as a shot.

Our goal is to show how insulin dosing and timing for meals is important, so we're focusing on rapid-acting insulin, which is usually taken at meal times.


Insulin Activity Curve

The insulin activity curve shows how effective the insulin is over time. It's important for understanding when to take insulin to best manage your blood sugar.


Rapid-acting insulin typically begins working 5 - 15 minutes after you take it, peaks within 30 - 90 minutes, and is active in the bloodstream for 2-5 hours.


Rapid-acting insulin has a smaller window and can cause bigger fluctuations to your blood sugar.



Disclaimer: Rapid-acting insulin works differently for everyone, so your activity curve may vary. Please refer to your healthcare provider for more information.


Bolus Timing

A bolus is insulin you take when you eat to keep blood sugar levels controlled after meals. Your bolus needs to act quickly to balance out the carbohydrates raising your blood sugar, so rapid-acting or short-acting insulin is typically used.


Pre-bolusing is when you take your insulin dose before you eat, usually 5-15 minutes before depending on the circumstances. Remember that rapid-acting insulin usually beings working 5-15 minutes after you take it, so pre-bolusing gives your insulin time to absorb in your body so that when you start eating, it’s ready to lower your blood sugar.


Your doctor will be able to advise you if you have any questions about when you should bolus. A doctor might recommend that someone take their insulin during or just after a meal if hypoglycemia needs to be prevented.


Disclaimer: There are many other factors that also affect glucose levels, such as exercising, food intake, illness, stress and medication. However, the focus for this tool is insulin.



Bolus Dosing

Different foods will affect your blood sugar differently, so your bolus dose needs to reflect what you're eating. Learning how to count carbohydrates (which raise your blood sugar) will help you figure out how much insulin to take to keep your blood sugar steady.


For this tool, we use a typical American meal as a standard, which usually has 45-75 grams of carbs. Different bodies will require different amounts of carbs, so these numbers are for this tool only.


Disclaimer: Depending on insulin sensitivity, you might need more or less insulin. The numbers in this tool are for example only; please ask your healthcare provider for more information.



Time In Range

Time-in-Range is the percentage of time your blood sugar levels are within a target range. Our tool features a target range of 70 to 180 mg/dL.


The goal is to increase your time spent in-range, while reducing the time spent below or above target range. Your time-in-range goals can be entirely specific to you. Time-in-range is a useful way to track your progress and to provide insights into what behaviors and choices affect your glucose values.


Disclaimer: Your blood sugar targets may be different depending on your age, any additional health problems you have, and other factors. Be sure to talk to your health care team about which targets are best for you.
